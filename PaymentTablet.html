RockSpoon’s payment tablet initially transformed the dining experience. However, we faced a wave of negative feedback from both restaurateurs and clients.

Recognizing the need for change, I spearheaded efforts to improve the application's usability and interface, ensuring a more accessible and enjoyable experience for all.

THE PROBLEM

Balancing Efficiency and Accessibility

Restaurateurs aimed to enhance the customer experience by enabling them to manage payments independently, thereby alleviating the burden on servers during busy periods.

However, this well-intentioned move faced resistance, particularly from elderly patrons, who encountered difficulties with the app's usability. The application, originally tailored for server usage, lacked the user-friendly features needed for a seamless customer experience, posing a challenge in the transition to self-service payments.

APPROACH

Starting with Questions...

We started a list of questions to explore how much room of improvement there is:

  1. How to close the gap between trained servers and the clients?

  2. How to hide the restaurant-related features that are available for the server from the clients?

  3. What range of client types might operate on the tablet?

  4. Will the client be able to understand the same UI that’s gonna be used by the server? Do we need two user interfaces?

  5. How to avoid having the client from calling the server to guide them on how to use the tablet?

Summary

Dynamic Payment Solutions

By offering two distinct interfaces and tailored experiences, we've crafted a dynamic payment application that caters to two distinct user profiles. Each interface is thoughtfully curated to address the unique needs of servers and clients. While the current iteration presents a robust solution, there's an exciting journey ahead as we launch the updated app, undoubtedly unveiling new challenges.

One intriguing consideration is the prospect of unifying the app experience and interface for both servers and clients. However, this raises questions about adjusting privileges – a delicate balance to strike. Should we streamline the interface, we must weigh the impact on server privileges or extend similar functionalities to clients.

Exploring ways to enhance client payment convenience while servers manage other tasks is another avenue worth investigating: One innovative idea involves implementing a QR code that clients can scan, redirecting them to their check in a web-based payment app. This personalized touch could elevate the overall user experience, offering a seamless and efficient payment process.

Moving Forward

I grew as a designer and walked away with a few key takeaways:

1

The Value of User Feedback

In the design journey, I discovered the enduring value of listening to user feedback. Even when aware of existing problems, user insights provide nuanced perspectives and can serve as a powerful tool to align the entire organization.

2

Pursuing Low-Hanging Fruit Embarking on the discovery

I initially underestimated the significance of the problem at hand. Pursuing what seemed like low-hanging fruit, I soon realized the depth of the challenge, unveiling not just a problem but a substantial opportunity waiting to be seized.

3

The Power of Systems Thinking

Adopting a systems mindset in design offers remarkable flexibility. Our transformation of a grid layout into grouped rows, guided by systems thinking at various levels (groups, headers, lists), resulted in a solution empowering our product management team to test large-scale hypotheses, while concurrently optimizing time for our engineering and design teams.
